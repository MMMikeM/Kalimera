# Stage 2: Habit Formation

The user has been at this for a few weeks. They've done it enough times that some
sessions happen almost automatically, but the habit isn't fully ingrained. Some days
they need willpower, other days they just do it. Roughly weeks 3-10.

## What's happening

The behaviour is transitioning from the dorsomedial striatum (deliberate, goal-directed)
to the dorsolateral striatum (automatic, stimulus-response). This is the neural
crossover. The user sometimes opens the app without thinking about it; other times
they still have to decide to do it. The habit is forming but fragile.

This is also where the automaticity curve starts to flatten (Lally). The large gains
from early repetitions are tapering off. Each additional session contributes less to
automaticity than the ones before it. This means the user may feel like "it's not
getting easier anymore" even though progress is real — they've just entered the
diminishing-returns phase of habit formation.

Critically, this is where external scaffolding should start pulling back. The user
who still needs a daily notification at week 8 hasn't formed a habit — they've formed
a dependency on the notification. The app's job shifts from prompting action to
reflecting competence.

## Good patterns for this stage

### Introduce the delta

**Why:** The user now has enough data for self-comparison to be meaningful. Showing
them their own improvement is the single strongest competence signal available,
and it's entirely self-generated — no external judge needed.

**Do this:**

- Show deltas at natural breakpoints (end of session, weekly summary, beginning of
  a new unit): "Two weeks ago, these forms took 9 minutes. Today: 4."
- Use multiple delta types to prevent any single metric from feeling stale:
  speed deltas (response time), volume deltas (items per session), difficulty deltas
  (complexity of material), accuracy deltas (error rate changes)
- Let users access a progress view when they choose, rather than forcing deltas into
  every completion moment. Some sessions the user just wants to finish and go.
- Only show deltas that tell a real story. Flat or declining metrics should be shown
  neutrally or not at all — never spun positively.

**Don't do this:**

- Fabricate encouragement when the data doesn't support it
- Show a delta after every single session (it becomes noise; save it for when there's
  a meaningful change)
- Compare to other users. The delta is self vs past-self, always.

### Taper external prompts

**Why:** The habit is forming. External prompts that persist at full intensity become
controlling rather than supportive. The user should be transitioning from "the app
reminded me" to "I just do this now."

**Do this:**

- Reduce notification frequency: every-other-day by week 4, twice-weekly by week 6
- Change notification content from prompting to informational: "You've practised 18
  days this month" instead of "Time to practise!"
- Offer explicit opt-out: "You've been consistent for 3 weeks. Want to turn off
  reminders?" Frame this as a compliment, not a risk.
- If the user misses a day, don't escalate. A single gentle prompt the next day is
  fine. "Your Greek is here when you're ready" not "You missed yesterday!"

**Don't do this:**

- Maintain daily notifications past week 3 unless the user explicitly asks for them
- Escalate notification urgency after missed days
- Guilt-trip: "We noticed you didn't practise yesterday..."
- Use loss framing at any point in this stage

### Let them set their own targets (now, not before)

**Why:** At Stage 1, goal-setting was premature — the user had no baseline. Now they
know what a session feels like, how long it takes, what's comfortable vs ambitious.
Self-set targets support autonomy because the standard comes from within, not from
the app.

**Do this:**

- Offer a lightweight goal-setting moment: "You've been doing about 10 minutes per
  session. Want to set that as your target, or adjust?"
- Let them change targets at any time without penalty or friction
- Frame targets as guides, not obligations: "Your target is 10 minutes" not "Goal:
  10 minutes (0% complete)"
- If they exceed their target, don't make a big deal of it. A quiet "12 minutes today
  (target: 10)" is enough. Let the satisfaction be theirs.

**Don't do this:**

- Impose targets the user didn't choose
- Penalise falling short of targets (reduced streaks, lost points)
- Celebrate target achievement with heavy UI (confetti, badges). The user set the
  target; meeting it should feel expected, not exceptional.

### Introduce desirable difficulty

**Why:** The user has enough baseline competence that challenge can be deliberately
introduced. Bjork's research: harder retrieval = more durable encoding. But this
only works if the user understands why it's harder — otherwise difficulty feels like
failure.

**Do this:**

- Increase spacing intervals (spaced repetition schedules should be widening)
- Interleave topics rather than blocking them (mixing tenses in a drill rather than
  practising one tense at a time)
- When difficulty increases, acknowledge it: "This drill mixes tenses. Mixing is
  harder, but research shows it produces more lasting memory."
- Use the struggle reframe actively: "These 4 forms took the longest today. The extra
  effort strengthens the memory trace."

**Don't do this:**

- Increase difficulty without explanation (feels like arbitrary punishment)
- Maintain easy difficulty to preserve high accuracy scores (optimises for the
  metric, not the learning)
- Reduce difficulty after errors without giving the user the option to stay at the
  harder level. Autonomy means they choose whether to push through or step back.

### Introduce domain bridges (sparingly)

**Why:** The user has enough knowledge that real-world connections start to be
meaningful. This creates relatedness — connection to the living domain — and
competence awareness — "I can actually use this."

**Do this:**

- After a unit or skill milestone (not after every session), connect what they've
  learned to something real: "You know enough vocabulary to read a Greek menu."
  "The tense you just practised is how news articles are written."
- Keep these infrequent enough to be genuinely interesting. Once a week maximum.
  More often and they become another form of noise.
- Make them specific to what the user has actually learned, not generic platitudes
  about the value of the domain.

**Don't do this:**

- Domain bridges after every session (becomes noise)
- Generic motivation: "Learning Greek is great for your brain!" (controlling,
  external-locus, and the user already chose to learn Greek — they don't need
  to be sold on it)

### Handle missed days with grace

**Why:** Lally's research is unambiguous: missing a single day does not significantly
impact habit formation. Duolingo confirmed this — Streak Freeze (forgiving missed
days) _increased_ engagement. Milkman's megastudy found microrewards for returning
after a lapse outperformed all other behavioural interventions.

**Do this:**

- After a missed day: nothing special. The app opens, the practice interface is
  there. Business as usual.
- After 2-3 missed days: a single, warm, no-guilt prompt. "Your Greek is here when
  you're ready. Pick up where you left off?"
- After a longer gap: acknowledge the gap factually, show what they'd built before
  the break, offer a lighter re-entry session. "You covered present and aorist
  tenses before your break. Want a quick review to refresh?"
- Never reset or penalise progress. The user's accumulated knowledge is real and
  shouldn't be taken away because they had a busy week.

**Don't do this:**

- "You broke your streak!" (loss framing, controlling, factually irrelevant to
  habit formation)
- "We missed you!" (parasocial, controlling, the app doesn't have feelings)
- Resetting XP, levels, or visible progress after a gap
- Increasing notification frequency after missed days (punishes the lapse with
  more controlling behaviour)

## Bad patterns for this stage

### Maintaining Stage 1 scaffolding intensity

**Why it's tempting:** It was working — the user kept coming back.
**Why it fails:** The scaffolding has become the cue rather than the activity itself.
The user practises because they got a notification, not because they want to. You've
trained dependency on external prompting. When the notification eventually fails to
fire (phone settings change, do-not-disturb mode), the behaviour doesn't happen.
The habit never actually formed.

### Escalating rewards to maintain engagement

**Why it's tempting:** The user's response to celebrations has flattened (prediction
error → zero). More elaborate celebrations seem like the fix.
**Why it fails:** This is the hedonic treadmill. Each escalation produces a briefly
larger prediction error, then habituates faster than the last. You end up with a
400-line particle animation component that still doesn't produce the hit that a
simple "well done" gave on day 1. The answer isn't bigger rewards — it's replacing
external rewards with competence-based informational feedback.

### Introducing competitive mechanics

**Why it's tempting:** Leaderboards and social comparison create strong engagement
signals in metrics.
**Why it fails at this stage:** The user is still building baseline competence.
Comparing them to others who've been at it longer (or who are faster learners) creates
a competence threat — the opposite of what they need. A 2023 Springer meta-analysis
found gamification enhanced perceptions of autonomy and relatedness but had minimal
impact on perceived competence. Leaderboards make people feel belonging (relatedness)
but not capable (competence). At a stage where competence-building is the primary
task, this is the wrong trade-off.

## The completion moment at Stage 2

This is where the full acknowledgement card pattern comes into its own:

1. **What happened:** "You practised 14 aorist forms and 8 present forms. 7 minutes."
2. **The delta** (when meaningful): "Average response: 1.8 seconds (was 3.4 two
   weeks ago)."
3. **Struggle reframe** (when the session was hard): "These 3 forms took the most
   effort. That's the retrieval that builds the strongest memory."
4. **The fork:** "Review the hesitations / Try these in conversation / Move to
   future tense"

Frequency: The full card on most sessions. On sessions where the user is clearly
in flow and wants to continue, just show the fork — or nothing at all. Read the
user's pace: if they completed the drill in 3 minutes and immediately want another,
don't break their momentum with a summary card. Let them keep going.
