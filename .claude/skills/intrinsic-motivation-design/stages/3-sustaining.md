# Stage 3: Sustaining

The user has been at this for months. The habit is established. They practise without
reminders, without streaks, without celebration. The question now isn't "how do I get
them to come back?" — it's "how do I support deepening mastery and prevent stagnation?"
Week 10 onwards.

## What's happening

The behaviour has transferred to the dorsolateral striatum. It's automatic. The user
doesn't deliberate about whether to practise — they just do. Notifications are
unnecessary or even irritating. The app is a tool, like a kitchen knife: you pick it
up when you need it without needing to be reminded it exists.

But automaticity has a shadow: the activity can become rote. The user goes through the
motions without genuine engagement. Sessions happen but feel empty. This is the
plateau — and it's where many long-term users quietly disengage. Not because they lost
the habit, but because they stopped growing.

The app's role at this stage is to facilitate depth, provide honest feedback about
genuine progress, support autonomy in charting new territory, and periodically connect
the user's growing competence to real-world capability. The app should be invisible
most of the time and genuinely useful when it appears.

## Good patterns for this stage

### Get out of the way

**Why:** The user doesn't need scaffolding, prompting, or encouragement. They need a
clean, fast, frictionless tool. Every unnecessary UI element, every unsolicited
celebration, every notification is friction for someone who already knows what they
want to do.

**Do this:**

- Minimise transition time between launching the app and doing the activity.
  Zero-tap-to-practice is the ideal: open app → you're in a drill.
- Remove or hide all motivational chrome (streak counters, XP displays, daily goal
  indicators) unless the user explicitly enables them.
- Session completion: often, just show the fork or load the next drill. No summary
  card needed unless there's a genuinely meaningful delta to show.
- Let the user configure what they see post-session. Some want the delta every time.
  Some want nothing. Autonomy means they choose.

**Don't do this:**

- Continue sending notifications to a user who hasn't needed them for weeks
- Surface "motivational" messages to someone who's clearly self-motivated
- Add new gamification features to re-engage long-term users (this signals the app
  doesn't trust that the activity itself is enough)
- Prompt the user to share achievements on social media. They're past the point where
  external validation serves them.

### Support deliberate practice, not just repetition

**Why:** At this stage, the user can do the basics automatically. More repetition of
easy material doesn't produce growth — it produces the plateau. Genuine progress
requires deliberately practising at the edge of current ability, which is less
comfortable than cruising through familiar territory.

**Do this:**

- Surface analytics that help the user identify their weak points: "Your accuracy on
  passive voice aorist is 60% vs 95% on active. Want to focus there?"
- Offer structured challenge paths: sequences that target specific weaknesses rather
  than random review
- Let the user design their own practice sessions: choose tenses, select difficulty,
  filter by error rate, create custom drills
- Make the spaced repetition algorithm transparent: "This card is due because you
  last got it wrong 4 days ago" not just "here's your next card"

**Don't do this:**

- Keep serving easy material to maintain high accuracy scores. This optimises for
  the metric at the expense of growth.
- Hide weakness data to avoid discouraging the user. At this stage, the user wants
  honest feedback — they're past the point where ego protection is more important
  than accuracy.
- Lock advanced practice behind completion requirements. A Stage 3 user should be
  able to jump to any area they want. They've earned full autonomy.

### Show mastery, not streaks

**Why:** The competence signal that matters at this stage isn't "you showed up 200
days in a row" (that's just attendance). It's "you can do things you couldn't do 6
months ago." The difference is between measuring input (sessions) and measuring
output (capability).

**Do this:**

- Cumulative competence views: total verb forms mastered, tenses covered, difficulty
  level progression over months
- Real-world benchmarks: "Your vocabulary covers ~70% of common Greek text."
  "You could understand a newspaper headline." These are genuine competence markers.
- Before/after comparisons at longer intervals: "Your first session in January: 3
  forms in 8 minutes. Yesterday: 24 forms in 5 minutes."
- Make these available on a dedicated progress page the user visits when they choose,
  not pushed into every session's completion moment.

**Don't do this:**

- Celebrate streak milestones (100 days! 200 days!). The user knows they've been at
  this a long time. A counter adds nothing they don't already feel.
- Display XP totals or level numbers. These are arbitrary and disconnected from
  actual ability.
- Compare them to other users. At Stage 3, the user is on their own path. External
  comparison is either irrelevant (different goals) or discouraging (someone is always
  further ahead).

### Offer new dimensions of challenge

**Why:** Growth mindset research (Dweck) shows that sustained motivation requires
the belief that improvement is always possible. If the app's content has a ceiling,
the user will hit it and leave. Depth is what retains Stage 3 users, not breadth
of gamification features.

**Do this:**

- Open new content types as competence grows: reading comprehension, listening
  exercises, free production (writing/speaking), cultural context
- Let users encounter the language in authentic contexts: real texts, real
  conversations, real media — not sanitised textbook examples
- If the app has user-generated content capabilities, this is the stage where they
  become most valuable. The user is competent enough to contribute, and contribution
  deepens mastery through the generation effect.
- Introduce meta-cognitive challenges: "Can you teach this form to someone else?"
  "Write your own example sentence." Teaching is the highest form of mastery.

**Don't do this:**

- Gate advanced content behind arbitrary milestones (500 XP to unlock, Level 10
  required). This is controlling and the user has earned access.
- Add difficulty ceilings. If the hardest drill is still too easy for the user,
  that's a content problem, not a motivation problem.

### Support identity integration

**Why:** SDT describes a continuum from external regulation ("I do this because the
app tells me to") to integrated regulation ("I do this because it's part of who I
am"). At Stage 3, the user is at or near integration — they identify as "someone who
learns Greek." The app's job is to reinforce this identity connection, not to layer
external mechanics on top of it.

**Do this:**

- Connect practice to the user's reasons for learning (captured during onboarding or
  inferred from behaviour). "You started learning Greek to read the New Testament in
  the original. Here's a verse that uses the tense you practised today."
- Celebrate qualitative capability transitions, not quantitative milestones: "You
  just completed your first drill entirely in aorist passive. That's a qualitatively
  different skill from where you started."
- Surface these rarely — once or twice a month at most. Frequent identity
  reinforcement feels manipulative. Rare, specific, genuine observations feel like
  insight.

**Don't do this:**

- Generic motivational messages ("Keep going! You're doing great!"). The user is past
  the point where this adds value. It feels like the app doesn't know them.
- Push social sharing prompts. Identity integration is internal. The user doesn't need
  external validation to maintain it.

## Bad patterns for this stage

### Re-introducing gamification to combat plateau

**Why it's tempting:** Engagement metrics dip as the novelty wears off. New
gamification features create a short spike.
**Why it fails:** The user is at Stage 3 because the activity itself sustains them.
Adding gamification at this point communicates: "We don't trust the activity to keep
you engaged." It insults the user's self-direction and introduces external-locus
mechanics into a context where internal locus was working fine. The engagement spike
from new features will habituate in days; the trust damage lasts.

### Retention-focused re-engagement campaigns

**Why it's tempting:** A Stage 3 user who takes a week off looks like churn in your
metrics.
**Why it fails:** They're not churning. They're living their life. A user with an
established habit who takes a week off for holiday doesn't need a series of escalating
emails ("We miss you!" / "Your progress is fading!" / "Come back before it's too
late!"). They'll come back when they're ready, and guilt-tripping them makes it less
likely, not more. The Milkman megastudy finding applies here: if you must do
something, a single warm "Your Greek is here when you're ready" after 7+ days is the
ceiling. Never escalate.

### Patronising milestone celebrations

**Why it's tempting:** 365 days feels like it should be celebrated.
**Why it fails:** The user has been at this for a year. They know. A confetti
animation for their 365th day treats them like they need external validation for
something that's become part of their identity. If anything, mark it with the same
quiet tone as everything else: "You've been practising for a year." Period. Let the
user feel whatever they feel about that without the app directing the emotion.

## The completion moment at Stage 3

At this stage, many sessions need no completion moment at all. The user finishes,
the next drill loads, they keep going or they close the app. The best completion
moment is the one that doesn't exist.

When something genuinely notable happens:

1. **Meaningful delta** (not every session — only when there's a significant change):
   "Your aorist passive accuracy crossed 90% this week. Three months ago: 45%."
2. **Capability milestone** (rare, genuine): "You've mastered all active voice
   forms across four tenses."
3. **Domain bridge** (occasional): "You could now parse most of the verb forms in
   a Greek news article."

These should appear inline, quietly, with no more visual weight than any other
text in the app. The user glances at it, feels what they feel, moves on or stops.
The app is the mirror, never the audience.

---

## The ultimate test

The best-designed learning tool is one the user eventually outgrows. If a user stops
using the app because they've achieved their goal — they can read Greek, they're fit,
they write daily — that's not churn. That's success. Design for that outcome rather
than against it. An app that makes itself unnecessary has done its job.
