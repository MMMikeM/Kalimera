# What to Skip or Defer

Organised by category. "Skip" means don't build into the app at all. "Defer" means don't build yet; there's a specific trigger for when it becomes relevant.

## Cases

### Skip
- **Vocative case in drills**: Greeks will understand you perfectly without correct vocative. The communicative cost of saying "Νίκος!" instead of "Νίκο!" is near zero. Exposure is fine; drilling is wasted effort.
- **Genitive plural of rare declension patterns**: Most genitive plurals in conversation are of a handful of common nouns. Drilling των θαλασσών is a waste of time.

### Defer
- **Genitive beyond possession**: Full genitive usage (after certain verbs, with prepositions, for age) is a Phase 4 concern. Until then, possession (του Νίκου) and pronoun clitics (μου, σου) cover 90% of genitive needs.
- **Full case paradigms for all nouns**: Start with accusative singular (highest production frequency), add genitive singular for possession, expand to plurals later. Don't try to fill all 8 cells (4 cases × 2 numbers) for every noun.

## Verbs

### Skip
- **Full passive voice paradigm**: Most passive forms in conversation are deponent verbs (verbs that look passive but aren't: θυμάμαι, κοιμάμαι). Teach those as individual verbs, not as "passive voice".
- **Past continuous / imperfect as a separate tense**: In early production, aorist covers past. Imperfect matters for nuance ("I was doing" vs "I did") but that nuance can wait. Introduce it incidentally when learners naturally need it for narrative.
- **Pluperfect**: Extremely rare in casual speech. Skip entirely.
- **Future perfect**: Even rarer. Skip entirely.
- **Full conjugation tables**: Never present a 6×4 (person × tense) grid. Teach specific person-tense combinations that come up in production prompts.

### Defer
- **Subjunctive as a named concept**: Don't teach "the subjunctive mood." Teach "after να and θα, the verb changes." The form matters; the label doesn't.
- **Imperative for uncommon verbs**: Start with 10-15 high-frequency imperatives (Έλα!, Κάνε!, Πες!, Φέρε!, Δώσε!, Κοίτα!, Σταμάτα!, Περίμενε!, Άκου!, Πάρε!). Expand only when these are automatic.
- **Verb aspect as an explicit concept**: Greeks use perfective/imperfective naturally. Learners acquire it through pattern exposure, not explanation. Don't build aspect drills; let it emerge from aorist vs present tense practice.

## Nouns

### Skip
- **Imparisyllable declension details**: Words like παππούς → παππούδες add a syllable in plural. Store them as irregular; don't build a rule system for them.
- **Archaic declension patterns**: Words that follow ancient Greek patterns (η οδός, η μέθοδος) are rare in conversation and usually only appear in fixed expressions or formal contexts.
- **Gender rules and exceptions**: Don't teach "nouns ending in -ος are usually masculine." Teach each noun with its article. The pattern is implicit.

### Defer
- **Noun plurals beyond common patterns**: Start with the regular patterns (-ος→-οι, -α→-ες, -ο→-α, -ι→-ια, -μα→-ματα). Irregular plurals can be added as individual entries when they come up.

## Adjectives

### Skip
- **Full adjective declension tables**: Don't drill all 24 cells (3 genders × 4 cases × 2 numbers). In conversation, you need nominative and accusative agreement. That's 12 cells, and many are identical.
- **Comparative/superlative formations as grammar**: Teach πιο + adjective as a chunk. Skip the synthetic comparatives (μεγαλύτερος) except for the 3-4 most common.

### Defer
- **Adjective agreement in genitive**: Until the learner is actively using genitive constructions, adjective agreement in genitive is pointless to drill.

## Pronouns

### Skip
- **Formal pronoun distinctions beyond εσείς**: Greek doesn't have a complex politeness system like Japanese or Korean. εσείς for formal "you" is enough.
- **ο οποίος / η οποία as relative pronoun**: που covers 90%+ of relative clauses in spoken Greek. Skip the declined relative pronoun entirely.

### Defer
- **Double object pronoun placement**: "Μου το δίνει" (genitive + accusative clitics) is a Phase 4 skill. The rules are complex and the payoff only comes when the learner is already producing both clitic types individually.
- **Strong vs weak pronoun forms**: Start with weak (clitic) forms only. Strong forms (εμένα, εσένα) are emphasis markers that come naturally later.

## Syntax

### Skip
- **Complex subordinate clauses**: Focus on coordination (και, αλλά, ή) and basic subordination (ότι, που, γιατί, αν). Complex syntax is a reading skill, not a production priority.
- **Word order rules**: Greek word order is pragmatically flexible. Don't teach rules. Let it emerge from exposure. SVO is the safe default.
- **Formal written register**: The app is for spoken production. Skip anything that's primarily written Greek (formal connectors, academic vocabulary, bureaucratic expressions).

### Defer
- **Conditional constructions**: Αν + imperfect for unreal conditions. Useful but not until the learner has imperfect tense available.
- **Reported speech**: Indirect speech transforms are complex and rarely needed in casual conversation.

## Phonology and orthography

### Skip entirely from the app
- **Spelling rules**: The app is for production (speaking), not writing. Don't drill ει vs οι vs η distinctions.
- **Phonological rules**: Don't teach assimilation, elision, or sandhi as rules. Teach the output forms.
- **Accent placement rules**: Store the correct accented form; don't teach the rules for where accents go.

## Metalanguage

### Skip
- **Grammatical terminology as a learning objective**: Never test whether the learner knows what "accusative" means. Test whether they can produce the accusative form.
- **Declension pattern names**: The learner doesn't need to know their noun is "third declension." They need to produce the accusative.
- **Aspect terminology**: Don't use "perfective" and "imperfective" in learner-facing content. Use "past (completed)" and "past (ongoing)" or just show the contrast through examples.

## The general rule

If you're unsure whether to include something, ask: **"Can I write a 5-second English→Greek production prompt that tests this?"**

If yes, it might belong in the app.
If no, it's either reference material or it should be skipped.
